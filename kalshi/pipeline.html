    Glossary — all terms defined
    Spread (bid-ask spread)
    The gap between the highest price a buyer will pay (bid) and the lowest price a seller will accept (ask). A spread of ≤ 3c means you can enter and exit near the fair price. A wide spread means you lose value just by placing the trade — before the outcome even resolves.
    Volume
    Total contracts (Kalshi) or USDC (Polymarket) traded in a market. Higher volume means more real money behind the price, making it a more trustworthy probability signal. A market priced at 99c with zero volume is just an illiquid placeholder — nobody has actually bet on it.
    Threshold (price)
    The minimum market price for a candidate to be considered. At 85c, the crowd already believes the outcome is ≥85% likely. The deep scan uses 80c to surface more candidates at the cost of more noise and false positives.
    Urgency Score
    A composite priority score combining three signals: 0.5 × time_score + 0.3 × prob_score + 0.2 × vol_score, each normalised 0–1. time_score = e^(−0.023 × days_to_close) — decays exponentially as the close date recedes. Used to select the primary representative when clustering and to sort candidates for the classifier. Higher score = more actionable right now.
    Market Clustering
    Grouping related markets by event_ticker before classification to avoid redundant LLM calls. Each cluster sends only its highest-urgency-score candidate (the "primary") to the classifier; the others ("siblings") are attached as context. Reduces the candidate count by ~55–68% on typical scans. A monotonicity check also detects logically inconsistent pricing within a cluster (e.g., an earlier deadline priced higher than a later one for a cumulative outcome).
    Time-to-Resolution Factor
    An adjustment to the CERTAIN classification threshold based on how many days remain until market close. Near-term markets (≤7 days) have little remaining surface area for surprises, so CERTAIN is easier to justify. Far-horizon markets (91–365 days) have more black-swan risk, policy changes, and new information — only structural impossibilities qualify. See classifier.html for the full tier table.
    Backtest Precision
    The fraction of CERTAIN-classified markets that actually resolved in the predicted direction. Target is ≥95%. Computed retrospectively by querying the Kalshi API for each market's settlement result and comparing it against the stored classification. Segmented by category, confidence band (e.g., 95–97 vs 98–100), and time-horizon tier.
    Recency Window
    The freshness window for the mandatory recency search — how far back the classifier looks for breaking developments. Default is 14 days. Override at runtime with the KALSHI_RECENCY_DAYS environment variable or adjust in config.yaml. Longer windows catch slower-moving stories; shorter windows focus on last-minute news.
    Edge
    Your expected return above the fair price. If an outcome is truly 99% likely but the market prices it at 94c, you have roughly 5% edge — for every $100 bet you expect ~$5 more profit than a fair bet would pay. Only positive-edge bets are worth making. A 3% minimum edge is required to trigger a notification.
    EV (Expected Value)
    The average outcome if you made the same bet many times under identical conditions. EV = (P(win) × profit if win) − (P(lose) × loss if lose). A positive EV means the bet is profitable in expectation — you will make money on average, even if any individual bet can still lose.
    Kelly Criterion
    A formula (J.L. Kelly Jr., 1956) that computes the fraction of your bankroll to bet for maximum long-run growth: f = edge × price / net_profit. We cap at 5% of bankroll because full Kelly is mathematically optimal only when your edge estimate is perfect. In practice, estimates have error — betting full Kelly on a bad estimate can cause large losses.
    Bankroll
    The total capital set aside specifically for prediction market betting. Kelly sizing uses this number to compute the maximum dollar amount to put on any single bet. Default is $1,000 — adjust in config.yaml.
    Annualised Edge
    Edge scaled to a yearly rate for fair comparison across different time horizons. Annualised = edge × (365 / days_to_close). A 4% edge closing in 30 days annualises to ~49%; the same 4% closing in 300 days is only ~5%. Shorter bets compound faster.
    Fee Rate
    The platform's cut on winnings. Kalshi uses a quadratic model averaging ~1.5%. This reduces your effective edge and is factored into all calculations. A 2% gross edge can turn negative after fees — always check the edge-after-fees figure in the report.
    True Probability
    The classifier LLM's assessment of the real probability of an outcome, expressed as confidence_score ÷ 100. This is distinct from the market price (the crowd's implied probability). The gap between true_prob and market price creates (or eliminates) positive edge.
    Settlement
    How the platform determines the final outcome and pays out winners. Settlement risk is when the platform might resolve the market differently than the real-world result — for example, using a specific lagging data source, ambiguous resolution criteria, or a discretionary call. Being factually correct is not always enough.
    Combo Exclusion
    Multi-leg bets that require several independent outcomes to all win. These show artificially high prices because the platform prices the compound probability. A 90% per-leg probability across 4 legs is only ~66% combined — these are excluded to avoid false signals.
    Volume Anomaly
    Unusually large trading on the opposite side from the obvious outcome. Could indicate informed traders ("smart money") who know something the market does not — or routine hedging. Worth investigating before acting on the "obvious" side.
    Smart Money
    Large, informed bets — typically from professional traders, insiders, or people with sophisticated research. When significant capital flows against the obvious outcome in a mid-probability market (20–79c), it signals the market consensus may be wrong. The anomaly scanner exists to surface these signals.
    Implied HC $ (High-Confidence Dollars)
    The dollar value implied by the consensus-side bet: volume × price / 100. Measures how much real money is backing the obvious outcome. Filters out markets that only appear certain because nobody has traded them.
    CERTAIN / LIKELY / UNCLEAR
    The three classifier output tiers. CERTAIN = near-mathematical certainty (≥95% confidence, ≥3 confirming signals, zero contradicting signals) — the only tier that proceeds to opportunity sizing. LIKELY = probable but not certain enough to act on. UNCLEAR = insufficient evidence.
    Confirming Signals
    Facts found during research that support the high-confidence outcome — news articles, official statements, statistical data, each with a source URL. CERTAIN requires ≥3.
    Contradicting Signals
    Facts that argue against the obvious outcome. Even one contradicting signal blocks a CERTAIN classification, forcing downgrade to LIKELY. Cluster pricing inconsistencies also count as a contradicting signal.
    Devil's Advocate Pass
    A built-in adversarial step where the LLM is explicitly asked for the strongest argument AGAINST its own CERTAIN conclusion. If it finds substantive counter-arguments, the classification is downgraded to LIKELY and a contradicting signal is added.
    Dedup / TTL
    Deduplication: suppressing repeat notifications for the same market. Once flagged, that market+side is suppressed for 7 days. TTL (Time-To-Live): how long the suppression lasts. After 7 days the entry expires and the opportunity can trigger a fresh notification if it still qualifies.
    Near-Miss
    A CERTAIN-classified market that was filtered before notification — due to a validation error, edge below 3%, or dedup suppression. Shown in grey on the Opportunities sheet so you can review what almost qualified.
    USDC
    USD Coin — a cryptocurrency stablecoin pegged 1:1 to the US dollar, running on the Polygon blockchain. Polymarket payouts arrive in USDC in a crypto wallet, not as direct USD. A separate cash-out step through a crypto exchange is required to convert USDC to spendable dollars.
